What I look for

A foundation problem isn't one problem. It's a family of problems with very different fixes and very different price tags. The visit is about figuring out which one you have.

  • Settling vs. heaving. Is the foundation going down or being pushed up? They look similar from inside and require opposite fixes.
  • Active vs. stable. Has the movement stopped, or is it still happening? Stable cracks get monitored. Active cracks get fixed.
  • Wall vs. footing. Is the wall failing in shear or is the footing failing in bearing?
  • Cause vs. symptom. Fixing the symptom without fixing the cause is the most expensive mistake homeowners make.

Common Corvallis foundation situations

1970s slab-on-grade homes with hairline cracks

Almost always cosmetic. Worth confirming with a diagnostic visit before considering significant repair work. Often the right move is a simple monitoring approach with a date-stamped photo and an inexpensive crack monitor from the hardware store.

Hillside homes with sloping floors

The Corvallis hills hide a lot of homes built on questionable cuts and fills. The right fix might be piering, might be wall reinforcement, might be drainage correction. The cost difference between those three approaches is significant. Get the diagnosis right.

Block foundations with stair-step cracking

Real and shouldn't be ignored. Solutions range from carbon-fiber straps for minor cases to full rebuild with piering for severe ones. The right answer depends entirely on the assessment.

The three things that kill deals

After 40 years of watching transactions, I can tell you that almost every dead deal dies for one of three reasons. A scary finding without a number, a bad-faith repair estimate from the wrong contractor, or a seller who hides something. I help with the first two.

1. Scary finding without a number

"There may be foundation movement." "Possible water intrusion in the crawlspace." Inspector phrases are deal-killers, not because the underlying issue is necessarily severe, but because uncertainty is what scares buyers. Get a real number and the conversation changes from "should I walk?" to "should I ask for a credit?"

2. A scope mismatch from the wrong type of contractor

When the inspector flags "possible foundation movement," the natural next call is a foundation specialist. The specialist is excellent at foundation work. But if the underlying issue is actually drainage, or settling that just needs monitoring, the result is a specialist-sized scope for a problem that may have called for a different answer. An independent assessment from a licensed GC who isn't tied to any single trade can confirm what the right scope actually is, and route the work to the best-matched specialist when work is genuinely needed. That gives buyer and seller a number both sides can work with.

3. The seller who hides something

Can't help with that one. But if you have a finding, get ahead of it. The Oregon residential disclosure form is unforgiving.

Common pre-listing situations I handle

  • Foundation findings on older Corvallis and Albany homes. We find the most efficient solution for the problem, no upsell.
  • Wet crawlspaces in Willamette Valley properties. Most older homes have water issues that should be evaluated holistically for grading, drainage and vapor control. We guide the process for the cleanest resolution.
  • Failed septic inspections in rural Philomath and Lebanon.
  • Roof, electrical, and mold concerns flagged by the inspector.
